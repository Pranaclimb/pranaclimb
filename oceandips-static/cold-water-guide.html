🌬️ Cold Water Guide

Everything you need to know about cold water immersion — the science, the benefits, the breath, and what to expect.

🌊 Why Cold Water?

Deliberate cold water exposure leads to an increase in attention and lifts your mood higher. It shifts your mental state.

There is a rapid increase of norepinephrine (530%), epinephrine (adrenaline) and dopamine (250%). The dopamine increase is sustained even after getting out — and mood stays elevated for long periods of time.

Cold water increases resilience, grit, our ability to move through a challenge, increases attention and mental performance, and improves our ability to regulate our mind and body under stress. The colder the water, the bigger the stress response — and the greater the adaptation.

Wim Hof: "Let the body do what the body is able to do. You are so much stronger than you think. The cold is real — it's a force, your inner power. Let it awaken."

2026 is Annie's 4th year going in the ocean, cold pools or ice baths every single day of the year. The benefits are instant:

  • Reduced pain and inflammation
  • Improved muscle recovery time after a workout
  • Reduces delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS)
  • Reduces rate of perceived exertion (RPE)

  • Noradrenaline concentration increases by 530%
  • Dopamine concentrations increase by 250%
  • Boosts immunity, metabolism and circulation

  • Feeling of being energised, increased optimism and well-being
  • Increases Natural Killer (NK) cells — cytotoxic immune cells with an innate capacity for eliminating cancer cells and virus-infected cells

🫀 What Happens in Your Body

Cold causes superficial blood vessels to tighten (constrict), forcing blood away from the skin and extremities toward the core to maintain body temperature. This reduces inflammation, swelling, and numbs nerve endings — which is why cold is used for acute injuries.

While blood is redirected to the core, it receives an influx of oxygen and nutrients — supercharging recovery and well-being.

When the cold stimulus is removed, blood vessels dilate (expand). The blood that was previously forced to the core — now packed with nutrients and oxygen — rushes back into the tissues. This aids recovery, reduces muscle soreness, and brings that wonderful feeling of warmth and energy.

When you remain completely still in the water, you will find you feel warmer. Your body is generating heat and that heat creates a thermal layer that surrounds your entire body — a bit like a wetsuit. When you move around you break up the thermal layer and feel colder. Moving is better for resilience.

Our head is not affected by peripheral vasoconstriction. Heat loss during cold water immersion is greatest in the head, neck, upper chest and groin.

Afterdrop is a continued cooling of the core temperature after getting out of the water. Research by Dr. Jørgen Melau found that core temperature is lowest around 15–30 minutes after exiting the water. This is why warming up immediately — drying off, layering up, and moving gently — is so important. Do not have a hot shower immediately.

❄️ Cold Shock — What to Expect

When you enter cold water, your body experiences a cold shock response. Understanding it removes the fear.

  • Gasp reflex — a sudden involuntary gasp
  • Rapid, uncontrolled breathing
  • Increased heart rate and blood pressure
  • Peripheral vasoconstriction in arms and legs
  • Blood volume centred to the core
Focus on your exhale. Slow down your breathing. Control is everything.

One of the first things you will learn to control as a cold water swimmer is the gasp reflex. If it happens: exhale with a strong 'fwaw'. This may feel counterintuitive but if you puff the air out, the next breath will come back in. Then concentrate on steadying your breathing rate.

Stay in the shallows until you can control your breath so that, if need be, you can stand up or walk back out.

There is a period of about 10–15 minutes when you first get into cold water where core temperature remains unchanged or even rises slightly. Use this time to settle your breath and ease into the experience.

🫁 The Breath Connection

The key to cold water is your breathing. This is where Ocean Dips connects with the Pranaclimb Methodology — using breath as a tool for control, performance, and resilience.

Through guided breathing, you learn to:

  • control the exhale
  • slow the breath rate
  • regulate your nervous system
  • stay calm in the cold
  • move through a challenge without panic

We do this by controlling our breathing. Cold water is not something to fight — it is something to breathe through.

✅ Before You Get In

  • Lay your clothes, towel, and dryrobe out in the order you want to put them on
  • Use layers — start with your core when dressing
  • Bring a hot flask of tea or warm drink
  • Stand on something to keep your feet insulated until you get in
  • Be strong in your mind
  • Let your body do what the body is capable of doing
  • Enter the water SLOWLY — never jump in
  • Control your breathing before swimming
  • Don't overstay your swim
  • Stay aware of sensations around your diaphragm and brain stem

⚠️ Do NOT jump in. Always enter slowly and in a controlled way. Sudden immersion in cold water can cause cold shock, which can be dangerous even for strong swimmers.

🔥 After Your Dip

  • Dry off immediately — pat, don't rub
  • Remove your wet swimsuit
  • Stand on something to insulate your feet
  • Dress with warm clothes — beginning with your core
  • Put a hat on immediately
  • Use a dryrobe — it's like being in a tent
  • Warm up naturally — a brisk walk or jog
  • Rehydrate — have your warm flask
  • Do not have a hot shower immediately — allow your body to rewarm naturally
  • Do not drive until you feel fully warmed and well

❓ Common Questions

There is no universal answer — it depends on your body. The popular "1 minute per degree Celsius" rule is a myth. Every body is different, and cold tolerance varies with sleep, health, stress, hydration, and what you've eaten. Start with short dips, see how you feel after you get out, and extend gradually. Make the journey into cold your own personal experiment.

Factors that affect tolerance include overall body mass, surface area to volume ratio, body fat, lifestyle, and day-to-day health. Be cautious, listen to your body, and always get out if something feels wrong.

As the body works to keep your core temperature up, it diverts blood away from the limbs to protect vital organs. Your muscles lose power, limbs become slow and heavy, and swimming becomes increasingly difficult. This is cold incapacitation. It is why swimming close to the shore, knowing your limits, and not overstaying your swim is so important.

Hypothermia is defined as when the body's core temperature drops below 35°C. It develops gradually — unlike cold shock. Signs include uncontrollable shivering, loss of coordination, weakness in arms and legs, clenched jaw, difficulty speaking, and hands becoming claw-like. In severe cases, the person may paradoxically feel warm — a dangerous sign.

Watch for "The Umbles" in yourself or others: Grumbles (negative outlook), Fumbles (poor coordination), Mumbles (slurred speech), Stumbles (loss of balance).

Acclimatisation is the process of becoming better able to deal with cold. The secret is simply to swim in cold water often — at least once a week, preferably two or three times, gradually extending the time. Get out if you are not comfortable. Don't set time goals.

It's easier to start when water is warmer (16°C+) and keep swimming as temperatures drop, rather than starting in winter. Making cold a way of life helps — lighter clothing, cooler showers, fresh air.

Cold water dipping without a wetsuit offers an immediate, exhilarating rush of endorphins — often referred to as a "cold water high". To do this safely, enter slowly to allow your body to acclimatise, focus on controlled breathing, and never swim alone.

🧤 Neoprene Gloves and Booties — these protect your extremities, which are often the first to feel pain or lose dexterity in cold water.

⏱ Keep Your Swims Short — as your body adapts to swimming without a wetsuit, start with short dips. Even if you feel like you could stay in longer, limit yourself — the effects of hypothermia can set in quickly. If you notice anything unusual such as feeling particularly tired or achy, exit the water immediately.

🔥 Warm Up Quickly — as soon as you exit, dry off immediately, layer up starting with your core, put on a warm hat, and have a hot drink ready in a flask. Sit somewhere warm and enjoy the post-swim glow!

As temperatures drop in winter, you may find it's colder changing in and out of a wetsuit than simply swimming in a costume and changing fast. Listen to your body and do what feels right for you.

As a general rule, seek expert medical advice before cold water swimming if you have a heart condition, high blood pressure, asthma, or are pregnant. Many people with minor medical conditions can take part safely — please disclose any conditions before your session so Annie can advise appropriately. Many people use cold water as a way of working with their health, not against it — but this journey is individual. If in doubt, always consult your GP first.

Do not use a hot shower or bath immediately after cold water swimming. Your body needs to rewarm naturally to avoid the risks of rapid temperature change. Dry off, layer up, move gently, and drink something warm. Scientists have observed that afterdrop occurs more frequently in swimmers who undergo rapid rewarming.
