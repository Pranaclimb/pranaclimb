Recovery Breathwork Protocol

Breathwork for Faster Climbing Recovery: The Protocol I Use

After a hard session, most climbers pack their bag and drive home. I spend ten minutes breathing deliberately — and it has changed how I feel the next day, the next week, and over a whole season. Here's exactly what I do, and why.

Annie Anderson
Founder, Pranaclimb · Breathwork Researcher
June 2025  ·  8 min read

Why Recovery Breathing Matters

Climbing is physiologically demanding in ways that are easy to underestimate. The combination of sustained isometric effort, repeated anaerobic bursts, and the psychological load of exposure creates a stress response that doesn't simply switch off when you unclip the rope.

Your heart rate may drop quickly. Your breathing rate may normalise within minutes. But your nervous system — specifically the sympathetic branch, the fight-or-flight system that fires hard during intense climbing — takes longer to settle. And if it doesn't settle properly, the downstream effects are real: disrupted sleep, slower muscle repair, higher cortisol, and reduced readiness for the next session.

Deliberate post-session breathing accelerates that shift from sympathetic to parasympathetic. It's not complicated. It costs ten minutes. The difference is noticeable.

"Your body doesn't know the climb is over. You have to tell it. The breath is how you do that."
Annie Anderson, Pranaclimb

What I Do — The Full Protocol

I developed this routine over several years, drawing on breathing physiology research, my own RBA data, and the practical reality of finishing a session at the crag or wall and needing something that actually works. It takes around ten minutes. It requires nothing except somewhere to sit or lie down.

The 10-Minute Post-Session Protocol

01
2 min

Cyclic Sighing

Inhale through the nose to about 80% of your capacity, then take a second short sniff to fully top up the lungs. Then a long, slow exhale through the mouth — twice as long as the combined inhale. Repeat for two minutes. This is the fastest-acting breath pattern for reducing stress and heart rate — more effective than any other single technique I've found.

02
3 min

Slow Nasal Breathing

After the cyclic sighing, let breathing normalise — but keep it nasal, and deliberately slow. Aim for around five to six breaths per minute if comfortable. This isn't box breathing with rigid counts; it's a gentle slowing that keeps you in the parasympathetic state. Three minutes here is enough to consolidate the shift.

03
3 min

Diaphragm Reset

Place one hand on your belly, one on your chest. Breathe so only the lower hand moves. The diaphragm takes a lot of load during hard climbing — it's both a breathing muscle and a postural stabiliser. Taking a few minutes of pure diaphragmatic breathing resets the pattern, reduces the accessory muscle tension that builds in your neck and shoulders, and reinforces the mechanics you want to carry into your next session.

04
2 min

Body Scan Breath

Two minutes of free, natural breathing while you scan from feet to head — noticing where you're still holding tension, and using the exhale to let it go. Forearms, shoulders, jaw, brow. This is the transition out of the protocol and back into the rest of your day. It's short. It matters.

The Science Behind the Choices

Why Cyclic Sighing First

Research from the Huberman Lab (Balban et al., 2023) found that cyclic sighing was the most effective real-time stress-reduction breathing technique when compared directly with box breathing and other protocols. The double inhale fully inflates the alveoli, and the extended exhale activates the vagus nerve — producing a measurable, rapid drop in heart rate and subjective stress.

For climbing, this is important because the first minutes post-climb are when the sympathetic nervous system is still running hot. Starting with cyclic sighing cuts through that quickly.

Why Slow Nasal Next

Slow breathing at around five to six breaths per minute has a well-documented effect on heart rate variability (HRV) — a key marker of recovery readiness. Nasal breathing produces nitric oxide, supports oxygen delivery, and maintains the calm state initiated by the cyclic sighing. The combination is more powerful than either alone.

Why the Diaphragm Reset

During hard climbing, the diaphragm shares its postural role with the effort of rapid breathing. Accessory muscles — the scalenes, the sternocleidomastoid, the upper trapezius — compensate. This is part of why climbers carry tension in their neck and upper back after a hard session. Resetting to pure diaphragmatic breathing reduces this residual tension and re-establishes the breathing pattern that supports both performance and recovery.

HRR₆₀ note: One of the things we track in the Pranaclimb RBA is your heart-rate recovery in the 60 seconds after a hard effort. Consistent post-session breathwork improves this metric over time — meaning you recover faster between attempts, which directly translates to more quality climbing per session.

What I Notice

I've been doing some version of this protocol for years. The most consistent thing I notice is sleep — specifically, how quickly I fall asleep on nights after a hard session, and how I feel the next morning. Before I had a deliberate routine, big sessions would leave me tired but wired — cortisol still elevated, sleep fragmented. The breathwork addresses that directly.

I also notice my forearms. Not that they stop being pumped — they do what they do. But the rate at which the pump clears on the drive home, and the residual fatigue in the days after, is genuinely different when I've done the protocol versus when I've skipped it.

This is anecdotal. Your experience will differ. But the physiology is sound, the time investment is minimal, and there's very little to lose by trying it.

A Note on Consistency

Like most things in training, the benefits of post-session breathwork are cumulative. One session won't transform your recovery. Ten sessions, done consistently, will. The protocol is short enough that the barrier to doing it is low — but only if you actually build it into your session rather than treating it as optional.

I do it in the car park. Or on a mat in the corner of the wall. Or on a flat rock at the crag. Wherever I am, as soon as I've packed my bag. It has become as automatic as putting my shoes away.

That's the goal. Not perfect technique. Just consistency.
