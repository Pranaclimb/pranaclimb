Breathing Toolbox

Your breathing toolkit

Each tool is mapped to a Pranaclimb performance zone. Use them strategically — before, during, and after your climbs.

Activation
🌬
Wim Hof Method

Progressive hyperventilation rounds followed by full retention holds. Accelerates VO₂ kinetics, primes CP, and builds CO₂ resilience before performance.

"Breathe deep, hold strong, rise."


Recovery
⬜
Box Breathing

4:4:4:4 equal-phase breathing. Resets the nervous system, manages CO₂ at rest holds, and restores W′bal between efforts. Ideal at CP zone.

"Inhale control, exhale calm."


Recovery
💨
Cyclic Sighing

Double inhale through the nose, long slow exhale through the mouth. Most effective breathwork for mood improvement and reducing physiological arousal (Huberman et al., 2023).

"Sigh it out — release and reset."


Recovery
🎈
Balloon Breathing

Deep nasal inhale, slow exhale into a balloon. Clinically shown to raise SpO₂ from 95.7% to 98.1% (Misra et al., 2023). Builds expiratory control and diaphragm strength.

"Fill it strong, release it slow."


Performance
⚡
Power Exhale

Forceful, sharp exhale at crux moments. Reduces inspiratory load, delays respiratory muscle metaboreflex, and releases core tension on hard moves. Above CP / RCP zone.

"Exhale sharp — drive through the crux."


All Zones
👅
Tongue-Y Balance

Rest tongue gently on hard palate, lips soft, teeth slightly apart. Promotes jaw and neck relaxation, supports nasal breathing, and reduces accessory muscle tension.

"Rest your tongue — soften your jaw, steady your breath."

Research Spotlight

The science behind it

Wim Hof · VO₂ Kinetics

Warm-Up Breathing & Endurance

A 17–22 min Wim Hof breathing warm-up altered VO₂ consumption during incremental cycling, potentially facilitating endurance performance and reducing subjective strain in young athletes. Average heart rates trended lower in preceded stages.

Bahenský et al., 2021

Cyclic Sighing · Mood

5-Min Breathwork vs Meditation

In a randomised controlled trial, 5 minutes of cyclic sighing produced greater mood improvement and reduction in respiratory rate than mindfulness meditation. Exhale-focused breathing was most effective for stress management.

Balban et al., 2023 (Huberman Lab)

Balloon Breathing · SpO₂

Oxygen Saturation Improvement

30 minutes of balloon-blowing exercise significantly raised SpO₂ from 95.7% to 98.1% in 250 patients. Activates the diaphragm and intercostals, enhancing alveolar ventilation and gas exchange efficiency.

Misra et al., 2023 — Cureus

Vocalisation · Ventilation

Yelling & Aerobic Power

Yelling at maximal effort led to higher peak power, time to exhaustion, and greater VO₂ and ventilation during 90–100% maximal cycling — without increased muscle activation. Supports Pranaclimb's +5 BPM scream adjustment rule.

Chen et al., 2015 — ResearchGate

Metaboreflex · W′bal

Respiratory Muscle Fatigue

Repeated Valsalva-type pressure raises intrathoracic pressure, activates the respiratory muscle metaboreflex, and causes vasoconstriction in limb musculature — accelerating W′ depletion. Validates the Scream-Density Override Rule.

Yamaguchi et al., 2020

Sighing · Reset

Sighs as Physiological Resetters

Spontaneous sighs act as physiological and psychological resetters, reducing tension and improving self-reported relief. Supports assigning a low or negative BR adjustment for sighs in Pranaclimb coaching.

Vlemincx et al., 2016

🎈 Balloon Breathing

Expansion Breath · Recovery Zone

What it is: Breathe deeply through the nose, then exhale slowly and steadily into a balloon, extending the exhalation as long as possible.

Why it works: Activates the diaphragm and intercostals, increases ribcage expansion, and strengthens expiratory control. Clinically shown to raise SpO₂ from 95.7% → 98.1% (Misra et al., 2023).

When to use: Recovery phases, off-wall sessions, between redpoint attempts.

"Fill it strong, release it slow — every balloon breath lifts your oxygen."

Research: Misra et al. (2023) — 30 min balloon breathing raised SpO₂ significantly in 250 patients. Supports use as a ventilatory training tool.

⚡ Power Exhale

Crux Tool · Above CP / RCP Zone

What it is: A forceful, sharp exhale timed to the hardest move on the wall. Think of it as a controlled burst — not a scream, but a deliberate pressure release.

Why it works: Reduces inspiratory load, delays the respiratory muscle metaboreflex (Yamaguchi 2020), releases core tension, and can enhance neural drive. Aliverti et al. showed that during phonation, all three chest-wall compartments contribute — shifting mechanics favourably at crux.

When to use: At crux moves, hard lock-offs, or when you feel the pump onset. Not for continuous use — use once, then reset with nasal breathing.

"Exhale sharp — drive through the crux."

⚠️ Scream-Density Rule: 1–3 vocalised power exhales = +5 BPM BR adjustment. ≥7 = metaboreflex activation risk. Budget them wisely.

👅 Tongue-Y Balance

Recovery Cue · All Zones

What it is: Place the tongue gently against the hard palate (roof of the mouth). Lips soft, teeth slightly apart. Breathe diaphragmatically through the nose.

Why it works: May promote jaw and neck relaxation, reduce accessory muscle tension, and support nasal breathing. Raises interoceptive awareness — pairs beautifully with Mirror Breath and Grounding Breath.

When to use: During ≤CP recovery holds, while route reading, or in post-crux grounding phases. Avoid during high-effort zones if it increases accessory muscle activity for you.

"Rest your tongue on the roof — soften your jaw, steady your breath."

Research note: Sherwin et al. (2024) reference Rocabado's recommendation for tongue-palate rest to reduce accessory muscle activity. Carlson et al. (1997) notes nuance — apply contextually.
