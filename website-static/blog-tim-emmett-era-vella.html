Elite Analysis Case Study Breathwork Performance

Tim Emmett &
Era Vella —
A Pranaclimb Perspective

Eight years. 130 days. One route. Tim Emmett's ascent of Era Vella 9a in November 2025 is one of the most documented examples of long-term physiological, psychological, and breathwork adaptation in climbing history — and it maps perfectly to the Pranaclimb methodology.

Annie Anderson
Researcher · Breathwork Specialist · Climber
By Annie Anderson · Pranaclimb 10 min read

The Story

Tim Emmett — a 51-year-old climber with three decades of elite experience — spent eight years projecting Era Vella, a 45-metre overhanging sport climb in Margalef, Spain. Approximately 130 days on the route. Tears, injuries, doubt, and emotional breakdown. And then, on November 16, 2025, he sent.

In the aftermath, Tim shared something remarkable — not just the send, but the preparation behind it. Cold water immersion. Mindfulness. Nutrition changes. And breathwork — including exhale breath holds of over four minutes, repeated five times in the days before his ascent.

"I've never been emotional with a climb… this one has had me in tears. I got into mindfulness and breathwork. I did this breathwork exercise a couple of days before I did the route. I held my breath for over four minutes on an exhale."
— Tim Emmett, post-send interview, December 2025

This is not just an inspiring story. Through a Pranaclimb lens, it is a masterclass in long-term physiological, psychological, and respiratory adaptation — every pillar of the methodology visible in action.

📅 Eight Years on the Wall — A Timeline

2017

Tim begins projecting Era Vella. Early efforts marked by high uncertainty, extreme body tension, elevated BR, and thoracic strain under sustained effort.

Nov 2025
The Send 🎉

Era Vella 9a — 2 days after a 5-round exhale breath hold session. Eight years. 130 days. The chains.

🔬 Tim's Breathwork — The Physiology

Tim's pre-send protocol is specific and physiologically significant:

Tim's Pre-Send Breathwork Protocol

  • 1️⃣ 2 minutes of hyperventilation (fast, deep breathing)
  • 2️⃣ Full exhale — breath hold
  • 3️⃣ Hold ≈4+ minutes
  • 4️⃣ Repeat × 5 cycles
  • 5️⃣ Performed 2 days before the send

This is not a standard warm-up protocol. This is a high-intensity CO₂ tolerance and psychophysiological resilience intervention. Here's what was happening inside Tim's body:

EffectMechanismWhy It Matters for Redpoints
🚀 Stress toleranceVery high CO₂ load late in the holdBuilds tolerance to crux panic and forearm burn — the Grey Zone
🧘 Parasympathetic reboundPost-hold vagal activationComposure and calm between attempts — faster HRR₆₀
🩸 Spleen primingSplenic contraction → ↑ RBC availabilityPotential short-term haemoglobin boost — more O₂ at the crux
🔥 Mental resilienceConscious discomfort toleranceTraining the brain to stay with the burn — not let go
🫀 Autonomic challengeCold water + breathwork combinationReinforces Calm and Recovery pillars simultaneously
Pranaclimb note on hyperventilation: Hyperventilation before a breath hold (as Tim used) drastically lowers CO₂, extending hold time — but is not recommended as a standard warm-up. Its value here is as a psychological and CO₂ resilience training tool, performed 2 days before the send — not immediately before climbing. Always practise safely.

🗺️ Mapping Era Vella to Pranaclimb Zones

Era Vella is a 45-metre, 5-star endurance testpiece in Margalef. Tim Emmett himself broke the route into four distinct sections — and each one maps precisely to Pranaclimb's CP/W′bal framework:

"The first bit is this vertical wall, about 5.12a. Then you do this V7-ish boulder problem around a roof to a no-hands rest. The next 22 moves are power endurance, maybe 8c/8c+. That's the tricky bit up to the twin pockets. After that, it's 45 moves of hard 5.13d."
— Tim Emmett, Climbing.com interview

The key insight: The twin pockets are not just a physical rest — they are a W′bal decision point. Climbers who arrive there with enough reserve send. Those who expended too much on the power endurance section fall on the upper 5.13d. This is exactly what 130 days of projecting teaches — how to pace the Grey Zone precisely enough to arrive at the twin pockets with fuel in the tank.
On the AI analysis above: Yes — this is essentially the Pranaclimb W′bal-ODE model being applied to Era Vella independently. CP, W′bal, RCP, recovery at rests — the Pranaclimb Methodology maps climbing physiology in exactly this way. It's validating to see the framework applied to real routes! 🧗‍♀️

🧠 The Psychological Load — and Its Breathing Cost

Tim's tears on the send are not just emotion — they are physiological data. The psychological load of an 8-year project carries real respiratory cost. Research by Seong Chin (2024) provides a powerful scientific explanation for exactly what Tim experienced in early attempts:

"Anxiety triggers muscle guarding, particularly in the torso, to stabilize against perceived threats. When outcomes are uncertain or don't match expectations — prediction errors — the brain signals more torso stiffness, fueling the anxiety cycle."
— Chin, S. (2024). The role of torso stiffness and prediction in the biomechanics of anxiety. Frontiers in Sports and Active Living.

In Tim's early attempts on Era Vella, this is precisely what was happening. The route was unpredictable — high uncertainty, steep exposure, repeated prediction errors. His body responded the only way it knew how: brace hard. And that bracing had direct physiological consequences:

🫁 Restricted Breathing

Torso stiffness compresses the diaphragm and thorax — directly limiting tidal volume, raising BR cost, and accelerating W′bal depletion.

❤️ Reduced Venous Return

Core bracing limits venous return to the heart, reducing cardiac output and slowing recovery between crux sequences.

⚡ Increased Perceived Effort

The same physical effort feels harder under anxiety-driven stiffness — RPE spikes even when the actual load hasn't changed.

🔄 The Vicious Cycle

Stiffness → restricted breathing → more anxiety → more stiffness. Familiarity and predictability are the only exits — which is exactly what 130 days of projecting builds.

The Pranaclimb insight: As Tim's familiarity with Era Vella increased — as the route became more predictable — posture softened, breathing became more rhythmic, and emotion shifted from threat to challenge. The breathing improvements weren't just fitness. They were the direct result of reduced torso stiffness through increased route predictability.
"That is the interesting thing about Era Vella. I've never cried on any route ever in 35 years of climbing. But Era Vella has brought me to tears multiple times. It's just a sport climb."
— Tim Emmett

💡 What Climbers Can Learn from Tim

🫁 Train CO₂ Tolerance

Tim's exhale holds directly trained the composure needed to survive the Grey Zone. Use the cMBT, BOLT or Huberman Protocol to build your own tolerance progressively.

🩸 Use Recovery as Training

Cold water immersion, nutrition, and sleep are not extras — they are performance. Tim's lifestyle changes in the final years were as important as his fingerboard sessions.

🧘 Regulate Emotion on the Wall

Fear and uncertainty have a direct breathing cost. Emotion regulation — labelling, reframing, committing — changes the physiological load on every attempt.

⚡ Understand W′bal

130 days taught Tim exactly how to pace W′bal across Era Vella's phases. You don't need 130 days — the Pranaclimb RBA maps your thresholds in 4 minutes.
