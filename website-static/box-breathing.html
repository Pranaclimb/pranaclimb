Breathing Toolbox · Recovery

Box Breathing

Equal-phase breathing — inhale, hold, exhale, hold — each for the same count. The simplest and most powerful tool for resetting your nervous system between efforts and restoring W′bal at rest holds.

Recovery At Rest Holds 4:4:4:4 CP Zone

How it works

The Method

Box breathing — also called 4:4:4:4 breathing — divides each breath cycle into four equal phases: inhale, hold full, exhale, hold empty. The equal duration of each phase creates a balanced, symmetrical breath cycle that powerfully activates the parasympathetic nervous system.

Used by Navy SEALs, athletes, and coaches worldwide, box breathing is the go-to tool for calming under pressure. For climbers, it's ideal at rest holds, between redpoint burns, or any time you need to bring heart rate down and restore your W′ reserve.

Step by step

How To Practice

  1. Find your rest positionSeated on a ledge, hanging in a rest hold, or lying down. Relax your shoulders and jaw completely.
  2. Inhale — 4 countsBreathe in steadily and evenly through the nose. Feel the belly and ribs expand. Keep it smooth — no gasping.
  3. Hold full — 4 countsHold with the lungs full. Stay relaxed — don't tense the throat or chest. Let the breath sit quietly.
  4. Exhale — 4 countsRelease slowly and evenly through the nose or slightly parted lips. Empty completely by the end of count 4.
  5. Hold empty — 4 countsHold with the lungs empty. Stay calm — this is where the parasympathetic response deepens. Then repeat.
  6. Continue for 6–12 roundsAs you settle in, you can extend each phase to 5 or 6 counts. Let the timer guide you.

Research Spotlight

The Science

Breathwork · Mood · Arousal

Box Breathing vs Mindfulness Meditation

In a randomised controlled trial comparing daily 5-minute breathwork practices against mindfulness meditation over one month, box breathing (equal duration inhale, hold, exhale, hold) produced significant improvements in mood and reduction in physiological arousal — including respiratory rate, heart rate, and heart rate variability. All breathwork conditions outperformed meditation for physiological outcomes.

Balban et al., 2023 — Huberman Lab / Cell Reports Medicine
