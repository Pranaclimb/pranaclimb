Wim Hof Breathing

Controlled hyperventilation followed by breath retention. Proven to accelerate VO₂ kinetics, build CO₂ resilience, and prime your body for peak performance on the wall.

Activation Pre-Climb CO₂ Resilience 4 Rounds

How it works

The Method

The Wim Hof Method uses controlled hyperventilation to manipulate the body's CO₂ and O₂ balance. By taking 30–40 deep power breaths, you temporarily reduce CO₂ levels and alkalise the blood — allowing a longer, more comfortable breath hold on the subsequent retention phase.

Each round ends with a recovery breath — a full inhale held for 15 seconds — before the next round begins. Over 3–4 rounds, the practice builds CO₂ tolerance, activates the sympathetic nervous system, and measurably improves oxygen uptake kinetics.

Step by step

How To Practice

  1. Get comfortableLie down or sit upright. Relax your jaw, shoulders and belly. Tongue rests gently on the roof of your mouth.
  2. Power breaths (30–40 reps)Inhale deeply through the nose — feel your belly, ribs and chest expand. Exhale passively, letting it fall out. Don't force the exhale. Keep a steady rhythm, about one breath every 1.5–2 seconds.
  3. Retention holdAfter the final exhale, let the breath fall out naturally and hold — don't force a full exhale. Hold for as long as is comfortable. This is your retention time.
  4. Recovery breathWhen you feel the urge to breathe, take one full deep inhale and hold for 15 seconds. Then release and begin the next round.
  5. Repeat for 3–4 roundsEach round you may find your retention time increases. Stay relaxed — never strain or force.

When To Use It

Pre-climb activation — 20–30 mins before a redpoint attempt or hard session
Morning practice — fasted, on waking, lying down for best results
Never in water — always practice in a safe, seated or lying position

Research Spotlight

The Science

VO₂ Kinetics · Endurance

Warm-Up Breathing & Performance

Incorporating a 17–22 minute Wim Hof breathing warm-up before endurance performance altered VO₂ consumption during incremental cycling exercise, potentially facilitating endurance performance and reducing subjective strain in young endurance athletes. Participants' average heart rates trended lower in stages preceded by the breathing warm-up.

Bahenský et al., 2021

Sympathetic Activation · O₂ Delivery

Hyperventilation & Sympathetic Response

Manipulation of breathing through a combination of hyperventilation and breath holding can elicit a sympathetic response that may improve circulating oxygen uptake. This connects directly to Pranaclimb's use of Wim Hof as a pre-climb activation tool — priming CP and accelerating VO₂ kinetics before the session begins.

Hof et al., 2017 · Lindholm & Gennser, 2005
